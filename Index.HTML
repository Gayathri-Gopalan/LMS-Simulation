🧬 DNA Replication Lab — Instruction Sheet
Prokaryotic DNA Replication (E. coli model) · Estimated time: 20–25 minutes

Overview

This interactive simulation models semiconservative DNA replication in prokaryotes (E. coli model). You will place each enzyme in the correct order, add nucleotides with correct base pairing, and observe how two identical daughter duplexes are produced from one original DNA molecule.

The simulation uses a 30-base sequence for visual clarity. A 5-question quiz follows completion to test your understanding.

Simulation simplifications (intentional): One replication fork is shown; real oriC replication is bidirectional — two forks move in opposite directions simultaneously. Okazaki fragments are 10 bases here; real prokaryotic fragments are ~1,000–2,000 nucleotides; eukaryotic fragments are shorter (~100–200 nucleotides, constrained by nucleosome spacing). The leading primer is placed near the template end for visual clarity; in reality it sits at the very 3′ tip of the top template strand.

Learning Objectives

  • Describe the role of each enzyme in prokaryotic DNA replication in the correct sequence.
  • Explain why DNA synthesis occurs only in the 5′→3′ direction and what this means for leading vs lagging strands.
  • Distinguish between the leading strand (continuous synthesis, one primer) and lagging strand (Okazaki fragments, one primer per fragment).
  • Explain why RNA primers are needed and how they are replaced by DNA via nick translation by Pol I.
  • Distinguish a nick (backbone break, nucleotides present) from a gap (missing nucleotides).
  • Define semiconservative replication and identify original vs newly synthesised strands in each daughter duplex.

Enzyme & Protein Reference

StepEnzyme / ProteinShape in SimFunctionKey Fact
Step 1 Topoisomerase
(DNA Gyrase — type II)		 Amber diamond — drag from toolbox Must be placed before Helicase. Relieves positive supercoiling ahead of the fork by cutting both strands of one DNA segment, passing an intact double-stranded segment through the break, then resealing. Slides ahead of the fork as unwinding progresses. Uses 2 ATP per catalytic cycle. Without gyrase, supercoiling ahead of the fork would stall helicase completely.
Step 2 Helicase
(DnaB in E. coli)		 Brown hexagon Loads at the origin of replication (oriC) and unwinds the double helix by disrupting hydrogen bonds between base pairs, using ATP. Continues translocating along the template throughout elongation — not only at the origin. In vivo, two helicases work bidirectionally. Uses ATP. A–T: 2 hydrogen bonds; G–C: 3 hydrogen bonds — GC-rich regions resist unwinding more. oriC is AT-rich to facilitate opening.
Step 3 SSB Proteins
(homotetramer)		 Teal 4-circle cluster Coat exposed single-stranded DNA to prevent re-annealing and protect from nucleases. Displaced progressively as Pol III synthesises each complementary strand — not all at once. E. coli SSB is a homotetramer (4 identical subunits). Released cooperatively as new strand grows.
Step 4 Primase
(DnaG in E. coli)		 Cyan circle Synthesises short RNA primers (4–12 nucleotides; shown as 4 here). Provides a free 3′-OH for Pol III to extend. One primer on the leading strand; one primer per Okazaki fragment on the lagging strand — re-initiated each time by helicase signalling. RNA uses U (uracil) not T. Only RNA polymerases can start a new chain de novo; DNA polymerases cannot.
Step 5 DNA Pol III Grey circle Main replicative polymerase — reads template 3′→5′, builds new strand 5′→3′. Leading strand: one continuous run from single primer. Lagging strand: each Okazaki fragment synthesised 5′→3′ away from the fork. Highly processive due to the β-clamp (sliding clamp). Real prokaryotic Okazaki fragments ~1,000–2,000 nucleotides (10 bases shown here for visual clarity).
Step 6 Nucleotides Coloured squares A/T/G/C Deoxyribonucleoside triphosphates added to the free 3′-OH of the growing strand. Each addition releases pyrophosphate, which is cleaved to drive the reaction forward. A pairs with T (2 H-bonds); G pairs with C (3 H-bonds). Each addition forms a phosphodiester bond between the 3′-OH of the chain and the 5′-phosphate of the incoming nucleotide.
Step 7 DNA Pol I Purple ellipse Nick translation: acts sequentially, one primer at a time. Its 5′→3′ exonuclease removes the RNA ribonucleotide at the 5′ side of the nick (in the direction of synthesis) while simultaneously extending from the 3′-OH with a deoxyribonucleotide. Also has 3′→5′ proofreading exonuclease. Leaves a nick — backbone break, no missing bases. Acts one primer at a time, processively through each primer, then dissociates. The nick it leaves is at the junction of the replaced primer and the adjacent downstream DNA.
Step 8 DNA Ligase Brown square Seals each nick by forming the final phosphodiester bond between the 3′-OH of one fragment and the 5′-phosphate of the next. Acts on the lagging strand nicks between Okazaki fragments, and on the leading strand nick at the primer/DNA junction. E. coli ligase uses NAD⁺ as cofactor (eukaryotic and phage ligases use ATP). Result: two complete, covalently continuous daughter duplexes.

Step-by-Step Guide

1Drag Gyrase (Topoisomerase II) to the right of the DNA
Must be placed before Helicase. Gyrase acts ahead of the replication fork, using 2 ATP per catalytic cycle to cut both strands, pass an intact double-stranded segment through the break, and reseal — relieving the positive supercoiling that would otherwise stall unwinding. Slides rightward as the fork advances.
2Drag Helicase to the DNA centre (oriC)
Helicase (DnaB) is loaded at oriC and unwinds the double helix using ATP. In vivo, two helicases load and move bidirectionally — one fork is shown here. Gyrase must already be active before helicase can proceed.
3Drag SSB proteins onto the exposed single-stranded DNA
SSBs (E. coli: homotetramer) coat all exposed single-stranded DNA to prevent re-annealing and protect from nucleases. They are displaced progressively — base by base — as Pol III synthesises the complementary strand.
4aDrag Primase — leading strand primer
Places the single RNA primer for the leading strand near the 3′ end of the top template. One primer only — leading strand synthesis is then continuous. Green-bordered bases = RNA (uses U not T).
4bDrag Primase — first Okazaki primer
Places the primer for Okazaki fragment 1 at the lagging strand fork (bases 1–4, nearest the fork). As each fragment completes, helicase signals Primase to re-initiate at the newly exposed fork — subsequent primers appear automatically.
5aDrag DNA Pol III — leading strand
Continuous synthesis, right→left. Pol III reads the top template 3′→5′ and builds the leading strand 5′→3′, following the fork. High processivity provided by the β-clamp (sliding clamp) — one primer, then uninterrupted synthesis.
5bDrag DNA Pol III — lagging strand
Each Okazaki fragment is synthesised 5′→3′ away from the fork. Pol III reads the bottom template 3′→5′ and builds each fragment left→right. Real prokaryotic fragments are ~1,000–2,000 nucleotides; 10 bases shown here for visual clarity.
6Add nucleotides A / T / G / C
Drag each nucleotide onto the gold-outlined position. Wrong base = rejected (A pairs with T; G pairs with C). Each correct placement forms a phosphodiester bond — pyrophosphate is released and cleaved to drive the reaction forward.
7Drag DNA Pol I
Nick translation: Pol I removes and replaces RNA primers one at a time, sequentially. Its 5′→3′ exonuclease removes the RNA ribonucleotide at the 5′ side of the nick (in the direction of synthesis) while simultaneously extending from the 3′-OH with a deoxyribonucleotide. Leaves a nick (not a gap) at each junction.
8Drag DNA Ligase
Ligase (uses NAD⁺ in E. coli) seals each nick by forming a phosphodiester bond between the 3′-OH of one fragment and the 5′-phosphate of the next. Seals nicks on both strands. Original strands dim — two complete daughter duplexes visible.

Key Scientific Concepts

Semiconservative replication: Each daughter molecule retains one parental strand (dimmed at completion) and gains one new strand. Proven by Meselson & Stahl (1958) in E. coli using ¹⁵N/¹⁴N density labelling in CsCl gradients.

Antiparallel strands & directionality: The top strand runs 5′→3′ left-to-right; the bottom strand runs 5′→3′ right-to-left (antiparallel — the two strands point in opposite directions). Pol III reads the top template right→left (3′→5′ of template) and synthesises the leading strand leftward (5′→3′). DNA polymerases can only add to a free 3′-OH — synthesis is always 5′→3′. This forces the lagging strand to be made discontinuously in short fragments, each primed separately at the fork.

Nick vs Gap: After Pol I, nucleotides are present at all positions but the phosphodiester backbone is broken at fragment junctions — this is a nick. A gap would mean missing nucleotides. Ligase seals nicks; it cannot fill gaps.

RNA primer necessity: DNA polymerases require a free 3′-OH to extend — they cannot start a new chain de novo. Only RNA polymerases (Primase) can initiate synthesis without a pre-existing strand.

Bidirectionality: In E. coli, two helicases load at oriC and two replication forks form, moving in opposite directions around the circular chromosome. This simulation shows one fork for clarity.

Okazaki fragment sizes: ~1,000–2,000 nucleotides in prokaryotes. In eukaryotes, fragments are shorter (~100–200 nucleotides), with spacing determined by nucleosome positioning. Each fragment requires its own RNA primer.

Glossary

oriC — origin of replication in E. coli; AT-rich ~245 base pair sequence where DnaA protein assembles and loads helicase
Okazaki fragment — short DNA segment (~1,000–2,000 nucleotides in prokaryotes) synthesised on lagging strand; joined by ligase
Phosphodiester bond — covalent bond linking 3′-OH of one nucleotide to 5′-phosphate of next; forms the DNA backbone
Hydrogen bond — non-covalent bond between complementary bases; A–T: 2 H-bonds, G–C: 3 H-bonds
Nick translation — Pol I starts at an existing nick and moves 5′→3′: its exonuclease removes the RNA ribonucleotide at the 5′ side of the nick while simultaneously extending from the 3′-OH with a deoxyribonucleotide, leaving a nick at the downstream junction when it finishes each primer
β-clamp (sliding clamp) — ring-shaped protein that encircles DNA and tethers Pol III, giving it high processivity
Semiconservative — each daughter duplex has one parental + one new strand; proven by Meselson & Stahl (1958)
SSB proteins — single-strand binding proteins; E. coli SSB is a homotetramer; stabilise single-stranded DNA, displaced progressively by Pol III
Topoisomerase (Gyrase) — active ATP-consuming enzyme (2 ATP per catalytic cycle); relieves positive supercoiling ahead of fork: cuts both strands, passes an intact double-stranded segment through the break, then reseals
Nick — a break in the phosphodiester backbone where adjacent nucleotides are not covalently linked; nucleotides are present (contrast: gap = missing nucleotides)
Template strand — the strand read 3′→5′ by the replicative polymerase to direct synthesis of the complementary new strand; both strands act as templates during replication
NAD⁺ (ligase cofactor) — E. coli DNA ligase uses NAD⁺; eukaryotic and phage ligases use ATP instead
